Hello everyone .

A long gap after my last post , but the algai sample still keeps me one place observing the diversity it is supporting from ciliates to rotifers and much more.

here is a macro photo of the wall from which i got the sample

Following in the post are a few more videos of differnt types of rotifers from this sample

All videos are shot using a 140x lens and maximum digital zoom of my cell phone.

  1. Following two videos have probably Bdelloid rotifers we can see the external morphology and a few internal details.

    in this video we can see that the rotifer has eaten some algae.(dont know what those circular things are)

     

  2. Next set of videos have a probable monogonont rotifer and the first one i dont know exactly what it might be . (Infact i dont know if any of my classifications is correct but i have tried based on what i could find …looking forward for corrections of my mistakes) . These like to move freely i.e without attaching themselves to anything , unlike many other rotifers,  so difficult to shoot as they are quicker but finally found one that was somehow not moving that fast

     

  3. This one i hardly managed to recognize as a rotifer . we can see its gut and many internal details and also the moving apparatus near its mouth part is clear enough . (Looking at it i initially thought that this might be the reason why its called a ROTIFERA..)

    the beating thing is that the wheel organ??

  4. The next video has all these three types of rotifers in the same field.

        In the past, I identified algae other than diatoms as well as zooplankton, and chironomid larvae in particular, so I started to think more expansively about what resources might be helpful. I have used Prescott’s book (don’t use the diatom section), albeit another old resource in terms of the taxonomy. Although these resources cover geographically diverse places, the morphology of the organisms (but not necessarily the taxonomy) will be similar across the board. Take a look at these resources and see what you think:

        1. Freshwater zooplankton: http://www3.laurentian.ca/livingwithlakes/wp-content/uploads/2012/06/Zooplankton-Guide-to-Taxonomy.pdf

        2. Chironomid pupae guide: http://midge.cfans.umn.edu/files/2012/09/Tortuguero-key-part-1.pdf

        3. Chironomid larvae guide: https://www.dpaw.wa.gov.au/images/documents/about/science/pubs/guides/guide-to-swwa-chironomidae-part-i.pdf

        3. How to know the fresh-water algae; an illustrated key for identifying the more common fresh-water algae to genus, with hundreds of species named pictured and with numerous aids for their study, G. W. Prescott, 1954 (https://archive.org/details/howtoknowfreshwa00pres).

        4. Gastrotrichs (http://www.caryinstitute.org/sites/default/files/public/reprints/thorp_covich_gastrotrichs_2010.pdf).

        5. microscopy-uk.org website has lots of information, pictures, and illustrations. For example, see: http://www.microscopy-uk.org.uk/mag/indexmag.html?http://www.microscopy-uk.org.uk/mag/wimsmall/algdr.html for information on desmids and http://www.microscopy-uk.org.uk/mag/indexmag.html?http://www.microscopy-uk.org.uk/mag/wimsmall/algdr.html on pond life. Also on the pond life page, click on the various links to see other organisms. This website also has an introductory guide on microscopy.

        6. Stream and Pond Field Trip: http://www.ableweb.org/volumes/vol-13/7-rahel.pdf
